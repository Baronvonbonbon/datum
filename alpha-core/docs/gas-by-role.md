### DATUM token mint emission (Path H — TOKENOMICS §3.3)

The protocol's intended emission model is **Path H: baked halvings + adaptive rate**.
Mirroring Bitcoin's two-layer design: outer halving + inner difficulty adjustment.

**Outer (baked, non-governable):**
- Total emittable supply: **95M DATUM** (after 5M founder premint).
- Halving period: **7 calendar years**.
- Epoch budgets (geometric): **47.5M, 23.75M, 11.875M, 5.94M, 2.97M, ...** Sums to exactly 95M.
- Daily cap derived: `epoch_budget / 2,555 days`. Epoch 0 = **18,591 DATUM/day**.

**Inner (dynamic adjustment, permissionless):**
- Per-DOT mint rate adapts every 1 day (governance-tunable [1, 90] days) to target the daily cap.
- Bounded: **MIN_RATE 0.001**, **MAX_RATE 200** DATUM/DOT.
- Max change per adjustment period: **2×** (anti-volatility).
- Bootstrap rate: 19 DATUM/DOT; adapts on first adjustment.

**Per-claim mint:** `totalMint = DOT_paid × currentRate`, capped by remaining daily +
epoch budgets. Split user 55% / publisher 40% / advertiser 5% (baked, not governable).

> **Implementation status:** Path H is **implemented** in `DatumEmissionEngine.sol`
> (commit de9a4a6+). `DatumSettlement` delegates the mint amount to the engine when wired;
> the legacy flat-rate path remains as a bootstrap fallback. Engine state, `rollEpoch()`,
> `adjustRate()`, and the per-batch cap clipping all match the spec.

### Advertiser ROI break-even

An advertiser pays CPM (cost per 1,000 impressions) and extracts value via clicks /
conversions / brand lift / attribution. Per-impression value (**VPI**) is the
fundamental economic input. Their break-even condition:

`VPI × imps_settled ≥ CPM × (imps_settled/1000) + setup_gas_DOT`

Solving for the maximum CPM they can pay while ROI ≥ 0:
`MaxCPM = 1000 × VPI − (setup_gas_DOT × 1000 / imps_settled)`

Measured setup gas per campaign: `createCampaign + vote = 806,104 gas`
= **0.00403 DOT** at 5 gwei. Amortises linearly across the campaign's settled imps.

### Minimum viable campaign size

Setup gas amortises over the campaign's settled imps. For the setup fee to be ≤ 5%
of the user-side minimum CPM at 5 gwei:

`setup_DOT × 1000 / N ≤ 0.05 × MinCPM_user` → **N ≥ 2,483 impressions**

Below this size, setup overhead becomes a meaningful share of the campaign's cost.
Above it, setup is structural rounding error. The recommended minimum campaign size
is therefore ~2k impressions — at $1 CPM that's a $12+ campaign budget.

### Advertiser economics with DATUM subsidy

When the advertiser routes part of their per-imp budget through DATUM rewards
(via `Campaigns.rewardPerImpression`), the cost accounting becomes:

`Total cost per imp = DOT_CPM/1000 + DATUM_reward_DOT_eq + setup/imps`
`ROI break-even: VPI ≥ Total cost per imp`

Whether the advertiser pays a unit of value in DOT or DATUM is a routing choice —
the **total per-impression compensation** is what matters. The economic floor is set
by VPI; the mix of DOT vs DATUM lets the advertiser shape the user-acquisition profile
(higher DATUM share = stronger network growth incentive; higher DOT share = clearer
market price signal).

**Key takeaway:** at any reasonable VPI ($0.001/imp and up — basically any campaign
with measurable conversion intent), the advertiser has 6×–60,000× of CPM headroom
above the user-side floor. Campaigns with VPI < $0.0001/imp (pure brand-awareness on
small audiences) struggle; everyone else has comfortable margins.

### 70-year halving projection

emission. Each halving compresses circulating-supply growth, which (under standard
monetary scarcity assumptions) tends to push price upward. The model is illustrative —
actual price paths are subject to demand-side dynamics, gas-market re-pricing,
and macro forces this exercise can't predict.

**Assumptions:**
- DATUM emission follows Path H: **7-year epoch halvings** with daily-cap-driven adaptive rate.
- 10 epochs span exactly 70 years; epoch n daily cap = 18591 / 2^n DATUM/day.
- DOT/USD growth: 2× per **DOT halving cycle** (assumed 4-yr — Polkadot's actual cadence).
- DATUM/USD growth: 2× per DATUM halving (7-yr) on supply scarcity alone.
- Baseline DOT = $5; baseline DATUM/DOT spot ratio = 0.10 (so DATUM ≈ $0.50 at year 0).
- Steady-state 1M-user network, 0.20 DOT CPM, monthly user batching, 3,650 imps/user/yr.
- Gas held at 5 gwei (no on-chain repricing modelled).

### Halving-projection findings

- **Programmatic-display first hits no-market under conservative growth**: year 36.
  Higher-VPI campaigns (retargeting and above) stay viable longer because their per-imp value
  is 10×–1000× higher.
- **Cap-depletion risk**: at default mintRate of 19 and 1M users, the 89M cap is hit in ~9 years.
  Halving extends this to perpetuity if asymptotic mint < cap.
- **DATUM mint subsidy stays constant in DOT-equivalent terms** under symmetric halving
  (mintRate halves, DATUM/DOT doubles → `mintRate × ratio` is invariant). The user's
  effective share stays at 1.42 for all 17 halvings.
  If DATUM/DOT grows **slower** than mintRate halves, the subsidy shrinks and the user-side
  floor rises; if **faster**, the subsidy expands and the floor falls.
- **Under aggressive DOT growth (4×/halving)**, programmatic display fails within ~8 years
  without gas repricing — the strongest argument for an on-chain gas-fee governor.
- **Optimal halving cadence trades off** scarcity narrative vs. user-side subsidy. Slower
  halvings (8-yr instead of 4-yr) preserve user subsidy longer; faster halvings amplify
  token scarcity. Governance lever via `setMintRate`.

### Long-run structural picture

Three forces compete on a 70-year horizon:

1. **DOT halvings** push DOT price up, increasing USD-denominated transaction fees
   (without on-chain repricing). This compresses the advertiser's USD margin.
2. **DATUM halvings** make each emitted token scarcer. If DATUM price tracks scarcity,
   token-unit emission halving doesn't reduce USD subsidy. If DATUM lags, subsidy weakens.
3. **Gas-market repricing** is the natural release valve: validators on Polkadot Hub
   could lower base fee (in DOT) as DOT price spikes, keeping real-world USD costs stable.

The dominant variable is whether **the Hub re-prices its gas fee curve**. If yes, DOT
halvings are absorbed by the chain and the user-side floor stays near today's level in
USD. If no, the chain becomes increasingly expensive over time and programmatic display
is the first ad category squeezed out.

**Strategic implications:**
- Bake gas-fee governance into the protocol roadmap (oracle-fed fee adjustments).
- Index DATUM rewards to USD via oracle so subsidy purchasing power is stable.
- Encourage longer user-batching cadence (quarterly+) as DOT price climbs to preserve
  the user-side margin without protocol intervention.
- Target high-VPI advertiser categories first; brand-awareness/low-VPI campaigns are
  the most fragile under DOT appreciation.

### Headline finding

With users self-settling and a monthly batching cadence at conservative Hub pricing,
the minimum CPM for the user side to be net-positive on gas fees is
**0.032 DOT per 1,000 impressions**. At $5/DOT that's ~$0.16 CPM
— over an order of magnitude below traditional web2 CPMs ($1–5).

### Practical implications

- **The user side dominates.** Publishers and advertisers break even at CPMs
  100–300× lower than what users need.
- **Higher-traffic users dominate proportionally less.** A user with 36k imps/yr
  breaks even at CPM ≈ 0.003 DOT — 10× cheaper than the 3,650-imp baseline.
- **Cadence is the lever, not contract optimisation.** Switching users from daily
  to monthly batching drops the break-even CPM 24×. Yearly batching drops it 80×.
- **At-scale viability is comfortable.** Real-world programmatic CPMs ($0.50–$5)
  sit 15–300× above the break-even floor at conservative pricing.
- **Worst-case (50 gwei × daily batching)** still bottoms out at CPM ≈ 7.3 DOT
  = ~$36/CPM — above traditional rates, but only in pathological combinations.
- The protocol's incentive design should encourage users to batch monthly+ via
  UI defaults and/or DATUM token rewards for delayed settlement.
